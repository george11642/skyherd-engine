# Lumpy Skin Disease (LSD)

## When to load

- Vision agent or drone camera detects raised skin nodules on cattle hide.
- HerdHealthWatcher cross-references a skin-lesion detection with this disease head.
- Any suspect skin lesion in cattle within the context of a foreign animal disease screening.

## Summary

Lumpy Skin Disease is a viral cattle disease (Capripoxvirus, LSDV) historically confined to Africa and the Middle East. It has spread into Europe (Balkans, 2015–2016), Asia (2019–2021), and is considered an emerging threat in the Western Hemisphere as of 2023. As of April 2026, LSD has not been confirmed in the continental US, but USDA-APHIS has issued enhanced surveillance guidance. Any suspected case in the US is a foreign animal disease emergency. It is spread by biting insects (mosquitoes, flies, ticks) and direct contact.

## Key facts

- **Pathogen**: Lumpy Skin Disease Virus (LSDV), *Capripoxvirus* genus. DNA virus; environmentally stable in dried scabs for weeks.
- **Host range**: cattle and water buffalo. Does not infect humans.
- **Transmission**: biting insects (primary in field settings); direct contact with nasal discharge, milk, semen; contaminated equipment.
- **Clinical signs**:
  - Firm, raised nodules 2–5 cm diameter on skin; distributed over body but concentrated on head, neck, limbs, and perineum.
  - Nodules may ulcerate; scabs form.
  - Fever 104–106°F for 1–2 weeks.
  - Nasal and ocular discharge; excessive salivation.
  - Limb edema, lymph node swelling.
  - Milk production drops 30–50% in dairy cattle; beef cattle show BCS decline.
  - Morbidity 10–20% typical; mortality 1–3% in naïve herds.
- **Regulatory status (US)**: Foreign Animal Disease — not present in US as of Apr 2026. **Mandatory immediate report** to state and federal authorities if suspected.
- **Differential diagnosis**: warts (papillomatosis), insect bites, ringworm, dermatophilosis — LSD nodules are larger, firmer, more uniformly distributed; fever is present.
- **No approved US vaccine**: killed and attenuated vaccines available in endemic regions. No licensed product in the US.

## Decision rules

```
IF multiple 2–5cm firm skin nodules detected on multiple animals:
  → DO NOT wait; Tier 4 page rancher immediately
  → File USDA-APHIS Foreign Animal Disease report (1-866-536-7593) within hours
  → Quarantine premises; no animal movement
  → Do NOT attempt treatment; await APHIS veterinarian

IF single nodule, unclear origin:
  → Rancher visual inspection; differential includes warts, insect abscess
  → If fever ≥104°F co-present, treat as LSD suspect until APHIS confirms

IF nodules only on face/head without systemic signs:
  → Lower suspicion; consider papillomatosis; rancher check recommended
```

## Escalation / handoff

- **Federal FAD hotline**: 1-866-536-7593 (24-hr USDA-APHIS).
