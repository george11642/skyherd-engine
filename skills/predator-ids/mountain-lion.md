# Mountain Lion (Puma concolor)

## When to load

- Thermal camera footage shows a large, low-slung felid with a long tail on or near ranch property.
- A carcass is found with signs consistent with a lion kill (clean throat bite, cached under debris).
- Drone is patrolling high-country or rocky terrain on the Gila, Sacramento, or Sangre de Cristo range margins.

## Summary

The mountain lion is the largest wild felid in New Mexico and the most dangerous single-predator threat to range cattle. Unlike coyotes, a lion targets adult cattle, not just calves, and a single lion can kill 1–3 animals per week on a home range. Lions are ambush hunters — they wait in elevated position and make a short explosive charge. Thermal imagery is the most reliable detection method at night; a lion will often remain motionless for extended periods near prey, which can cause misidentification as a rock or log. NM has an estimated population of 3,000–4,500 lions with highest densities in the Gila Wilderness and Sacramento Mountains.

## Key facts

- **Weight**: 80–220 lb (36–100 kg); males substantially larger than females.
- **Shoulder height**: 24–30 inches; body length (with tail) 5–8 ft.
- **Thermal signature**: large, elongated body with long tapering tail. Tail length = body length is a reliable felid indicator vs. canid. Low-slung posture; crouching heat signature often appears as an irregular blob against warm rocks or soil at night.
- **Gait**: fluid walking gait; diagonal leg movement (like domestic cat); rare direct trot unless in open field. Stalk-and-freeze pattern — moves in short bursts, stops completely for 30–120 seconds.
- **NM range**: year-round in Gila Wilderness (Grant/Catron/Hidalgo counties), Sacramento Mountains (Otero county), Sangre de Cristo Mountains (Taos/Santa Fe/Mora counties). Lower density in eastern plains and central NM.
- **Activity**: primarily nocturnal; crepuscular. Rarely seen in full daylight except in remote terrain.
- **Vocalization**: screams and yowls during mating (Jan–Mar); otherwise largely silent. Audio detection is unreliable for routine monitoring.
- **Hunt behavior**: ambush from rocks, tree branches, or elevated terrain. Approaches downwind. Single decisive throat/neck bite to collapse trachea or break neck.
- **Kill sign**: clean bite to throat or base of skull; often partially consumed; carcass covered with grass, dirt, or brush ("cached"). Lion frequently returns to feed over multiple nights.
- **Livestock impact**: mountain lion attacks on cattle are less frequent than coyote but per-incident economic loss is far higher. Each kill averages $800–$1,500 on NM range (USDA-APHIS Wildlife Services).
- **Legal status NM**: game animal; hunting regulated by NM Dept of Game and Fish. Lethal removal requires a depredation permit from NM DGF or USDA-APHIS Wildlife Services.

## Decision rules

```
IF thermal signature >80 lb felid form with long tail detected:
  → Presumptive lion; Tier 3 call rancher immediately
  → Drone to 90m AGL (higher than coyote protocol — lion can react aggressively to low drone)
  → Log GPS with timestamp for PredatorPatternLearner

IF felid form within 200m of herd after dark:
  → Tier 3 call rancher; activate deterrent (low-frequency; see deterrent-protocols.md)
  → Continue monitoring; do not approach below 60m AGL

IF cached carcass found (drone visual — covered with debris, partially consumed):
  → Tier 3 call rancher; lion returning tonight is likely
  → Recommend moving remaining herd >500m from kill site tonight

IF lion within 50m of herd or visible at dawn:
  → Tier 4 immediate; rancher armed response may be needed
  → Begin depredation documentation for NM DGF permit

IF large cat observed near rock outcrop or ridgeline at distance >300m:
  → Log; Tier 1; lion may be transiting; no immediate livestock threat

NEVER activate acoustic deterrent in high-frequency coyote band for lion:
  → Cross-reference deterrent-protocols.md; lion deters differently (low-frequency rumble)
```

## Escalation / handoff

- **Tier 3**: any confirmed lion on property or within 500m of herd.
- **Tier 4**: kill confirmed or lion inside herd.
- NM DGF Depredation Program: 1-800-432-4263.
- USDA-APHIS Wildlife Services (NM): 505-346-2640.
